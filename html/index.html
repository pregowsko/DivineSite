    ChatGPT is a large language model developed by OpenAI that can be used for natural language processing tasks such as text generation and language translation. It is based on the GPT-3.5 (Generative Pretrained Transformer 3.5) model, which is one of the largest and most advanced language models currently available.

    One of the key features of ChatGPT is its ability to generate human-like text responses to prompts. This makes it useful for a wide range of applications, such as creating chatbots for customer service, generating responses to questions in online forums, or even creating personalized content for social media posts.

    To use ChatGPT, you will need to have access to the OpenAI API. Once you have obtained an API key, you can use the ChatGPT model by sending it a prompt in the form of a text string. The model will then generate a response based on the information it has been trained on.

    ChatGPT uses deep learning algorithms to generate text responses to prompts. The model is based on the GPT-3 architecture, which is a type of transformer model that uses self-attention mechanisms to process and generate text.

    The GPT-3 architecture is a type of neural network that is composed of multiple layers of interconnected nodes. Each node in the network is designed to process a specific aspect of the input text, such as the overall meaning, the syntactic structure, or the contextual information. As the input text is passed through the network, the nodes work together to generate a coherent and grammatically correct response.

    The development of the GPT-3 architecture was led by Alec Radford, Jeffrey Wu, Rewon Child, and David Luan, who are all researchers at OpenAI. The team worked closely with a number of other researchers and engineers at OpenAI to develop and train the GPT-3 model on a large corpus of text data.

    Touchless 3D printing with sound

    Read More

    Research teams from Germany's Max Planck Institute for medical research and the University of Heidelberg have developed a way to 3D print objects in one "piece" using sound waves. Their technique, which is described in the journal Science Advances, uses profiled ultrasonic waves to create acoustic holograms that exert pressure on the printed material like an invisible form.

    The group used their technique to 3D print objects from micro-beads of hydrogel beads and even biological cells - all completely 'touch free'. As Heiner Kremer, who wrote a program that prints with this technique, explains in the publication on the research, the transition from a 3D model to the working ultrasonic sphere was quite difficult, requiring the use of a custom algorithm.

    Most importantly, the sound waves do not damage cells, which means that no chemical auxiliaries are needed, and consequently the technique can be particularly useful in 3D bioprinting of tissues and cell cultures. Ultrasonic waves can also penetrate deep into tissues, which allows them to be manipulated in a less invasive way.

    Metasurfaces in displays

    Read More

    Researchers from British and Australian universities have developed a display technique based on silicon metasurfaces, reporting their findings in the journal Light: Science & Applications. Metasurfaces are thin layers of material with nanoscale patterns that can manipulate light in unusual ways.

    It is believed that solutions of this kind could replace LCD layers in flat-screen TVs. The technology can also be used for dynamic virtual holography, in LiDAR technologies and for the production of thinner flat panels with a resolution 100 times higher than current LCD-based screens, while reducing energy consumption by up to half.

    "The capabilities of conventional displays have reached their peak and are unlikely to improve significantly in the future due to numerous limitations," says Dragomir Neshev, professor of physics at the National University of Australia, in the paper.

    "The search for a high-resolution, fast refresh rate all-solid-state flat panel display technology is currently underway. We have designed and developed meta-surface pixels that, unlike liquid crystals, do not require polarized light to function, which will cut the screens' power consumption in half."

    To control individual pixels with high modulation rates, the researchers' demonstration platform uses transparent conductive oxide as an electrically driven heater that can rapidly change the optical properties of silicon metasurface cells, which are a hundred times thinner than liquid crystal cells. The result of this method is a response time of less than one millisecond, which according to the publication is ten times the limit of detection for the human eye.

    Physicists on the way to 'quantum light'

    Read More

    According to a paper published in Nature Physics, an international team of physicists has successfully manipulated photons, the elementary particles of light, for the first time. Although it may not sound particularly sensational to the layman, physicists describe it as a great quantum breakthrough that opens up huge scientific and technological possibilities.

    While physics is already adept at controlling quantum entangled atoms, achieving the same with light has proven much more difficult. In a new experiment, a team from the University of Sydney and the University of Basel in Switzerland fired a single photon and a pair of bound photons at a quantum dot (an artificially created atom) so that they could measure the immediate time delay between the photon itself and those that were bound.

    Stimulated emission and detection of single photons as well as small groups of photons in a single atom was achieved, leading to their strong correlation, in other words 'quantum light'.

    'By demonstrating the manipulation of photon bound states, we have taken an important first step towards harnessing quantum light for practical applications,' explains Sahand Mahmoodian of the University of Sydney in a release. The next steps are to use this approach to build better quantum computers.
